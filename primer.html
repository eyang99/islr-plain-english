Primer · foundations

Probability & Statistics, finally

The stuff every ML book assumes you remember. Not a full course — the minimum you need so the rest of this site doesn't feel like a foreign language.

The one-sentence version

Probability is forward. Statistics is backward.

Probability: "here's the coin, predict the flips." Statistics: "here are the flips, guess the coin." Every formula in this section serves one of those two jobs.

1. Random variables & distributions

Random variable

A number whose value depends on something you can't fully predict. "How long until my Uber arrives" is a random variable. "The temperature at noon tomorrow" is a random variable. "2+2" is not.

A random variable is a factory that stamps out numbers. The distribution is the recipe that says which numbers are common and which are rare.

Distribution

A map from "possible value" → "how likely." Draw the histogram you'd get if you ran the factory a million times. That's the distribution.

2. Expectation & variance

Expected value E[X] — the center of gravity

If you ran the random variable a million times and averaged the results, that's the expected value. It's the long-run mean. It does not have to be a value the variable can actually take — the expected value of a die roll is 3.5, which no die ever shows.

Expectation is where the distribution balances on a pencil. Not the most common value — the balance point.

Variance — how spread out

Average squared distance from the mean. Small variance = tight around the mean. Big variance = wild. We square the distances so negatives don't cancel positives.

Standard deviation is just the square root of variance, put back in the original units. That's why everyone quotes σ, not σ².

Formulas

$$ E[X] = \sum_x x \cdot P(X=x) \quad\text{(discrete)} \qquad E[X] = \int x f(x)\,dx \quad\text{(continuous)} $$

$$ \mathrm{Var}(X) = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 $$

Useful rule: $\mathrm{Var}(aX+b) = a^2\mathrm{Var}(X)$. Shifting doesn't change spread; scaling scales it quadratically.

3. Covariance & correlation

Covariance

Do two variables move together? If X is above its mean when Y is above its mean (and below when below), covariance is positive. If they move opposite, it's negative. If they're unrelated, it's zero.

Correlation

Covariance is in weird units (X-units times Y-units). Correlation divides by both standard deviations so it lives in [−1, +1] — unitless, comparable across problems.

Correlation is not causation — you know this. But also: zero correlation is not independence. Correlation only measures linear relationships. Y = X² has zero correlation with X over a symmetric range, but they're perfectly dependent.

4. The Central Limit Theorem

The single most useful result in statistics

Take any distribution (skewed, ugly, whatever) with a finite mean and variance. Pull n samples from it. Take the average. Do that many times and plot those averages. As n grows, the distribution of averages looks like a bell curve. Every time. Regardless of what you started with.

The CLT is why averaging washes out weirdness. One flip of a weird coin is weird. The average of 100 flips looks Gaussian no matter how weird the coin.
n = 1

Drag n up. Watch the ugly skewed parent distribution become bell-shaped as you average more samples.

Why you care for ML: Every confidence interval, every p-value, every standard error in the book leans on this theorem. It's what lets you say "my average is probably within ±2 standard errors of the truth" without knowing the truth.

5. Sampling, estimators, standard error

Population vs sample

Population: every possible observation. Usually you can't see it. Sample: the subset you actually measured. Stats is the art of saying something about the population using only the sample.

Estimator

Any formula that takes a sample and spits out a guess about the population. The sample mean is an estimator of the population mean. It's also a random variable — a different sample gives a different estimate.

Standard error

The standard deviation of the estimator — how much your guess would bounce around if you re-did the whole study. Not the same as the standard deviation of the data. For the sample mean: $SE = \sigma/\sqrt{n}$. More data → tighter estimate, shrinking as $\sqrt{n}$, which is why doubling accuracy costs 4× the data.

Common confusion: "Standard deviation" measures spread in the data. "Standard error" measures spread in your estimate. They have different symbols and different meanings. ML docs abuse this constantly.

6. Confidence intervals

"My estimate is 5.2 ± 0.4 (95% CI)" means: if I re-ran this study many times, 95% of the intervals I'd construct this way would contain the true value. It does not mean "there's a 95% chance the truth is in this specific interval" — the truth is fixed, the interval is random.

A CI is a net. The fish (the true value) is sitting still. You throw many nets. 95% of them catch the fish. You're looking at one net — did this one catch it? You don't know. You just know the method works 95 times out of 100.
Formula for mean

$$ \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} $$

For 95%, $z \approx 1.96$. If $n$ is small, use $t$-distribution instead of $z$.

7. Hypothesis tests & p-values

The logic

You suspect something (alternative H₁). You set up a strawman — nothing is happening (null H₀). You ask: if the strawman were true, how surprising is my data? That "how surprising" number is the p-value.

p-value, in one sentence

The probability of seeing data at least this extreme, assuming the null is true. Small p = my data would be weird if the null were true → reject the null. Big p = my data is consistent with the null → don't reject.

p < 0.05 does NOT mean: "there's a 95% chance my hypothesis is true," or "the effect is big," or "the result matters." It only means "this data would be rare if nothing were going on." Chapter 13 is devoted to why this gets abused.

8. Bayes' rule — the update machine

Before you see evidence, you have a guess (prior). After you see evidence, you have a new guess (posterior). Bayes' rule tells you how to update.

$$ P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)} $$

In English: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.

Your prior is your starting opinion. The likelihood is "how well does this evidence fit each possible world?" You multiply them and normalize. Done.

The classic medical-test gotcha

A disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A test is 99% accurate both ways. You test positive. What's the chance you actually have it?

Most people say 99%. Actual answer is about 9%. Why? Start with 10,000 people. ~10 have the disease; the test catches ~9.9 of them. Of the 9,990 healthy people, 1% test positive — that's ~99.9 false alarms. So out of ~109 positives, only ~10 are real. The prior (how rare the disease is) dominates.

ML connection: Bayes' rule is the backbone of naive Bayes classification (Ch 4), and the philosophical foundation of Bayesian ML. Any time you see "prior" or "posterior," this formula is under the hood.

9. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Given a blob of data and a family of candidate distributions, MLE picks the distribution under which your observed data would have been most likely. Turn the knob (the parameter) until the density curve hugs your points as tightly as it can.

You see a scatter of darts. MLE asks: "of all the possible dartboards and throwing styles, which one would most likely have produced this pattern?" Pick that one.

Almost every model in this book is fit by some form of MLE (linear regression with Gaussian errors, logistic regression, etc.) or by a penalized version (ridge/lasso add a penalty to the likelihood).

10. The five-word summary

If you close this tab remembering only this:

  1. Distribution = the recipe for the number factory.
  2. Mean = balance point. Variance = average squared spread.
  3. CLT = averages go Gaussian, even when the data doesn't.
  4. Standard error ≠ standard deviation. SE shrinks as $1/\sqrt{n}$.
  5. p-value = P(data this weird | null is true). Not P(null is true | data).
  6. Bayes = posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. Priors matter more than your gut thinks.

Why square the deviations for variance?
So negatives don't cancel positives, and so big deviations are penalized more than small ones. SD = √variance puts it back in the original units.

The distribution of the sample mean approaches a Gaussian as n grows, regardless of the shape of the underlying distribution (given finite mean/variance).

Standard deviation vs standard error?
SD = spread of the data. SE = spread of the estimator (like the sample mean). SE = SD / √n.
6
What does a 95% confidence interval actually mean?
If you re-ran the study many times, 95% of the intervals would contain the true parameter. It's a property of the method, not of this specific interval.
7
Define p-value.
Probability of seeing data at least this extreme IF the null hypothesis were true. Not the probability the null is true.
8
Type I vs Type II error?
Type I: reject true null (false positive, crying wolf). Type II: fail to reject false null (false negative, missing the wolf). α = Type I rate, β = Type II rate, power = 1 − β.
9
Bayes' rule in words.
Posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. Update your belief by multiplying your starting belief by how well each hypothesis fits the evidence.
10
What does Maximum Likelihood do?
Picks the parameter values that make the observed data most probable. Twist the knob until the density curve hugs your points tightest.
11
Why doesn't zero correlation imply independence?
Correlation measures LINEAR relationships only. Y = X² has zero correlation with X over a symmetric range but is perfectly dependent.
12
You test positive on a 99% accurate test for a disease affecting 1 in 1000. Chance you have it?
≈ 9%. Low base rate (prior) dominates. Out of 10,000: ~10 sick, 9.9 true positives; ~9990 healthy, ~99.9 false positives → ~10/110 real.
