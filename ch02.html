Chapter 2 · the most important chapter

Statistical Learning

If you understand this chapter, the rest of the book is mostly variations. Bias-variance, training vs test error, KNN. Read this one twice.

Big idea

Every model's total error splits into three pieces. Two you can fight. One you can't.

Bias (too simple to see the truth) + Variance (so flexible it chases noise) + Irreducible noise (the universe's coin flips you'll never predict). The whole game of ML is juggling bias against variance. The noise stays.

1. What we're actually trying to estimate

We assume there's a true function $f$ linking inputs to outputs, plus some noise:

$$ Y = f(X) + \epsilon $$

We don't know $f$. We have a dataset $(x_i, y_i)$ for $i = 1, \dots, n$, and we want to build $\hat{f}$ to approximate $f$.

Reducible vs irreducible error

Total error when we predict $\hat{Y} = \hat{f}(X)$:

$$ E[(Y - \hat{Y})^2] = \underbrace{(f(X) - \hat{f}(X))^2}_{\text{reducible}} + \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}(\epsilon)}_{\text{irreducible}} $$

A better model shrinks the reducible part. Nothing shrinks the irreducible part. Any paper claiming zero test error is either lying, overfit, or living in a freakishly noise-free world.

The irreducible error is the wind. No matter how perfect your aim, the dart moves on the way. The reducible error is your aim. You can get better at aim. You can't make the wind go away.

2. Parametric vs non-parametric methods

Parametric

Assume a functional form for $f$ (e.g., linear: $f(X) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \dots$). Then the problem reduces to estimating a few numbers (the $\beta$s). Fast, interpretable, can be very wrong if the form is wrong.

You walk into a tailor and say "I want a suit." The tailor says "I only make rectangles." Cheap, fast, but if your shape isn't a rectangle, tough luck.

Non-parametric

Make no strong assumption about the form of $f$. Let the data shape the model (e.g., KNN, trees). Much more flexible, needs more data, harder to interpret.

A bespoke tailor traces your body. Fits beautifully, but they need to see a lot of you to get it right, and they can't describe the pattern in two sentences.

3. Training error vs test error

The distinction that breaks beginners

Training error: how well you fit the data you've already seen. Test error: how well you'd predict new data you haven't seen. Only the second one matters.

A model with zero training error can be a disaster on test data. "Memorize every training answer" is a strategy that hits zero training error and fails completely in the real world. That's overfitting.

4. The bias-variance decomposition

The expected test MSE at a point $x_0$ decomposes:

$$ E[(y_0 - \hat{f}(x_0))^2] = \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}(\hat{f}(x_0))}_{\text{variance}} + \underbrace{[\mathrm{Bias}(\hat{f}(x_0))]^2}_{\text{bias}^2} + \mathrm{Var}(\epsilon) $$

What bias and variance actually mean

  • Bias: if I re-trained on many different samples, what's the average error from the truth? High bias = the model is systematically wrong because its assumed form is too simple.
  • Variance: how much would the model change if I trained it on a different sample of the same size? High variance = the model is jumpy — sensitive to which points it happened to see.
Bias = you always hit the wrong spot on the dartboard, even though your throws are tight. Your aim is calibrated wrong.
Variance = your throws are scattered all over, sometimes hitting bullseye by luck. Your aim is unstable.

5. K-Nearest Neighbors — your first model

The dumbest model that works. To predict at a new point $x_0$:

  1. Find the $K$ training points closest to $x_0$.
  2. Average their $y$ values (regression) or take the majority vote (classification).
  3. That's your prediction.

No training phase. The "model" is the dataset itself. The only knob is $K$.

The K knob is a bias-variance knob

  • Small K (K=1): every prediction copies the nearest training point. Super flexible. Low bias, high variance. The decision boundary is jagged and changes wildly if one training point moves.
  • Large K: average over many neighbors. Smoother, more stable. Higher bias, lower variance. In the extreme (K=n), you predict the global average every time.
K = 5

Two-class KNN. Small K → wiggly boundary (overfit). Large K → smooth boundary (risk of underfit).

KNN regression formula

$$ \hat{f}(x_0) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{x_i \in N_K(x_0)} y_i $$

where $N_K(x_0)$ is the set of the $K$ training points nearest to $x_0$ (usually by Euclidean distance).

6. Classification: the 0/1 loss and Bayes classifier

For classification, accuracy is measured with the error rate — the fraction of predictions you get wrong.

The theoretically best-possible classifier is the Bayes classifier: at each $x$, predict the most probable class given $x$. Its error rate is the Bayes error rate — the classification equivalent of irreducible error.

You can't compute the Bayes classifier in real life (you don't know the conditional probabilities). But it exists in theory, and every method is judged by how close it gets.

7. The curse of dimensionality

Methods like KNN die in high dimensions. Why? In high dimensions, every point is far from every other point — "nearest" loses meaning. The number of data points you need to cover a space grows exponentially with the number of features.

In 1D, filling a line with points is cheap. In 2D, you need a grid — much more. In 100D, the universe's worth of data wouldn't fill it. So "closest neighbor" in 100D is basically random.
This is why Ch 6 (regularization) and Ch 10 (deep learning) exist. You either throw features away, or you use methods that don't care about distance the way KNN does.

The single mental model for this chapter

Every supervised model sits somewhere on a knob between "too rigid" and "too flexible." The ideal spot — the bottom of the U-shaped test error curve — depends on how much data you have, how noisy it is, and how complex the truth really is. The rest of the book is a catalog of models, each with their own knob, each with their own trade-off.

Flashcards

1
Decompose expected test MSE at a point.
Bias² + Variance + Irreducible noise. Reducible = first two. Noise is the floor you can never go below.
2
What is bias in ML?
The error from a model's structural assumptions being too simple or wrong. High bias = systematically off, even with infinite data.
3
What is variance in ML?
How much the fitted model changes if you re-train on a different sample of the same size. High variance = jumpy model, overfit.
4
Why is training error misleading?
A flexible enough model can memorize the training set (zero training error) while generalizing terribly. Only test error tells you if the model learned the signal.
5
Parametric vs non-parametric — short version.
Parametric: assume a form (like a line), estimate parameters. Fast, interpretable, can be badly wrong. Non-parametric: let the data shape the fit. Flexible, data-hungry, harder to interpret.
6
Describe KNN in four lines.
1) To predict at x₀, 2) find the K training points closest to x₀, 3) average their labels (or vote), 4) return that. No training phase. K is the only knob.
7
How does K in KNN move the bias-variance knob?
Small K = low bias, high variance (wiggly, overfit). Large K = high bias, low variance (smooth, may underfit). K=n predicts the global average.
8
What is the Bayes classifier?
The theoretically optimal classifier: at each x, predict the class with the highest conditional probability. Its error rate (Bayes error) is the classification floor.
9
State the curse of dimensionality.
As dimensions grow, data points all become roughly equidistant. Distance-based methods like KNN break down because "nearest neighbor" stops being meaningful.
10
Why does test MSE often form a U-shape vs model flexibility?
Flexibility reduces bias (model fits truth better) but raises variance (more sensitive to the specific training sample). The sum is U-shaped; the minimum is the sweet spot.
