Chapter 6

Model Selection & Regularization

You have 500 predictors and 200 observations. Now what? Three strategies: pick fewer, shrink all of them, or project to a new space.

Big idea

When you have too many knobs, the model fits the noise. The cure is to either remove knobs or penalize big ones.

Subset selection tries to keep only the useful predictors. Shrinkage (ridge, lasso) keeps all of them but pulls their coefficients toward zero. Dimension reduction (PCR, PLS) builds a smaller set of directions that capture the signal.

1. Why not just least squares?

Least squares minimizes RSS. If you have enough data relative to predictors (n much larger than p), this works beautifully. But things go south when:

  • p is close to n: variance of the coefficients explodes.
  • p > n: the solution isn't even unique (infinite lines fit perfectly).
  • Correlated predictors: coefficient estimates bounce around wildly.
  • Many irrelevant predictors: they contribute variance without helping bias.

Solution: give up some bias to reduce variance, dramatically.

2. Subset selection

Best subset

Try every possible subset of predictors. For each size k, keep the best. Then choose across sizes using CV or an information criterion. Becomes computationally impossible past p ≈ 30 (2^30 models).

Forward stepwise

Start empty. Add the predictor that most improves fit. Repeat. Cheap, greedy, but can miss good combinations where a pair of predictors only works together.

Backward stepwise

Start with all predictors. Drop the least useful one at each step. Requires n > p. Similar greedy problem.

Picking the right size

You can't use training RSS or R² — they monotonically improve with more predictors. Use instead:

  • Cp, AIC, BIC: RSS + penalty for model size. Different penalty strengths. BIC penalizes harder, picks smaller models.
  • Adjusted R²: accounts for degrees of freedom.
  • Cross-validation: most reliable, no distributional assumptions. Just slower.
BIC vs AIC: BIC prefers parsimony (smaller models), AIC is more generous. Neither is universally "right" — BIC is better if you believe the true model is among your candidates; AIC if you're doing pure prediction.

3. Ridge regression — shrink toward zero

Instead of minimizing RSS alone, minimize RSS plus a penalty on the size of the coefficients:

$$ \min_{\beta} \; \mathrm{RSS} + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j^2 $$

$\lambda \geq 0$ is the tuning parameter. When $\lambda = 0$: ordinary least squares. When $\lambda \to \infty$: all coefficients shrink to zero. Pick $\lambda$ via cross-validation.

Ridge says: "I don't trust a model that leans hard on any one coefficient. Shrink them all toward zero. The ones that really matter will fight back and stay larger."

Why it helps

Shrinkage reduces variance (less sensitive to which sample you got) at the cost of a little bias. For correlated predictors especially, ridge stabilizes wildly fluctuating OLS estimates.

Always standardize your predictors before ridge or lasso. Otherwise the penalty punishes variables with large numeric scales more than variables with small scales — which is arbitrary nonsense.

4. Lasso — shrink and select

Same idea as ridge but with an L1 penalty instead of L2:

$$ \min_{\beta} \; \mathrm{RSS} + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p |\beta_j| $$

The key difference: ridge's L2 penalty shrinks coefficients toward zero but never exactly to zero. Lasso's L1 penalty can set coefficients exactly to zero. That means lasso does automatic variable selection.

Ridge is a fader — it turns every coefficient down proportionally. Lasso is a bouncer — it kicks weak coefficients all the way out the door and keeps only the strong ones.

5. Ridge vs Lasso — which to pick?

  • Many of the true coefficients are truly zero (sparse truth) → lasso wins. It does the selection for you.
  • All predictors contribute a little (dense truth) → ridge wins. Lasso would throw away genuinely useful variables.
  • Correlated predictors where you want only one → lasso will (arbitrarily) pick one and zero the rest.
  • Want to keep correlated predictors together → elastic net (a mix of ridge and lasso) or group lasso.

Don't know which truth you're in? Run both, cross-validate, pick the winner. Or use elastic net (α = 0 is ridge, α = 1 is lasso, α in between blends).

6. Dimension reduction: PCR and PLS

Different idea: build new predictors that are linear combinations of your originals, and use a small number of these combinations.

Principal Components Regression (PCR)

Step 1: run PCA (Ch 12) on the predictors to find directions of greatest variance. Step 2: regress Y on the first M components. Step 3: tune M via CV.

Catch: PCA looks only at X, not Y. There's no guarantee that directions of high X-variance are also directions that predict Y. They usually are in practice. When they're not, PCR disappoints.

Partial Least Squares (PLS)

Same structure as PCR but the components are chosen to have high covariance with Y, not just high X-variance. In theory this should help. In practice, PLS and PCR are often similar, and lasso/ridge usually dominate both.

7. High-dimensional data (p > n)

Genomics. Text. Images before deep learning. You have more features than observations.

  • OLS is undefined (infinite solutions fit perfectly).
  • R² is meaningless — can be pushed to 1.0 with garbage.
  • You must use shrinkage, subset selection, or dimension reduction.
  • Report test error via cross-validation, not training fit.
  • Even then, be honest about "multicollinearity at scale": coefficients aren't identifiable, so don't read the story too confidently.

Mental model

OLS says "do the best I can with the data." Ridge says "do the best I can, but prefer small coefficients." Lasso says "do the best I can, and actively kill the weak ones." PCR/PLS say "throw the whole thing into a smaller basis first." All are trading a dab of bias for a big drop in variance. That's the free lunch of regularization.

Flashcards

1
Why does OLS break down when p is close to or exceeds n?
Coefficient variance explodes, and when p ≥ n, least-squares has infinitely many solutions fitting training data perfectly. Regularization or subset selection is required.
2
Describe forward stepwise selection.
Start with an empty model. At each step, add the single predictor that most improves fit. Stop by CV or an information criterion. Greedy, cheap, can miss good combinations.
3
Write the ridge penalty.
λ · Σβⱼ² (L2). Added to RSS. Shrinks coefficients smoothly toward zero but never to exactly zero.
4
Write the lasso penalty.
λ · Σ|βⱼ| (L1). Can set coefficients exactly to zero → automatic variable selection.
5
Why standardize predictors before ridge/lasso?
The penalty treats all coefficients equally. If one predictor is measured in thousands and another in fractions, their coefficients are on vastly different scales and the penalty becomes scale-dependent and meaningless.
6
Ridge vs lasso — sparse vs dense truth?
Sparse truth (few nonzero coefficients): lasso wins. Dense truth (many small contributions): ridge wins. Unsure: try both with CV, or use elastic net.
7
How is λ chosen?
By cross-validation. Fit ridge/lasso over a grid of λ values; pick the λ that minimizes CV error.
8
PCR vs PLS — what's the difference?
PCR picks components by maximizing X-variance (ignores Y). PLS picks components by maximizing X-Y covariance. PLS is theoretically smarter; in practice they often tie.
9
What is elastic net?
A penalty that mixes L1 and L2: α·|βⱼ| + (1−α)·βⱼ². Gets lasso's selection with ridge's grouping for correlated predictors.
10
Why can't you trust coefficients from a p ≫ n fit?
They're not identifiable — many different coefficient vectors fit equally well. The model may predict well, but "which variable matters" is not a sensible question without much stronger assumptions.

Quiz

Shrinkage is a philosophy before it's an algorithm.

Question 1 · compute (ridge extremes)

In ridge regression, the penalty is $\lambda \sum_j \beta_j^2$. As $\lambda \to \infty$, what do all the $\hat{\beta}_j$ (excluding the intercept) approach?

Solution

$\mathbf{0}$. An infinite penalty on any nonzero coefficient forces every coefficient to zero. The prediction becomes a constant (the intercept). At $\lambda = 0$ you're back to ordinary least squares — so $\lambda$ is a continuous dial between "full OLS" and "null model."

Question 2 · explain

You have 1000 genes and 80 patients. Your postdoc runs a plain linear regression and gets a model with huge $R^2$. Why are you worried?

Model answer

$p \gg n$. With 1000 predictors and only 80 observations, OLS is not identified in the usual sense — once you have 80 columns linearly independent of the rest you can perfectly fit the 80 $y$-values. $R^2$ near 1 is a mathematical artifact, not a sign that the model learned anything. Out-of-sample error will be terrible. The entire chapter is about how to proceed: subset selection is too combinatorial at $p = 1000$, so you reach for ridge or lasso (or PCR/PLS) to get stable estimates. Cross-validate $\lambda$ on patient-level splits (not gene-level).

Key pointsp > n makes OLS meaningless; $R^2$ is an artifact; regularize + CV.

Question 3 · explain

Ridge and lasso both shrink coefficients. Why does lasso give you sparse coefficients (many exactly zero) and ridge doesn't?

Model answer

Geometry of the penalty. Ridge's constraint region is a hypersphere (smooth, no corners); the elliptical RSS contours of the loss typically hit it on a face, so no coefficient is forced to exactly zero — each just gets small. Lasso's constraint region is a hypercube rotated 45° — a diamond with sharp corners on the axes. The RSS contours usually first touch the diamond at a corner, where one or more coordinates are zero. That's why lasso produces sparse solutions: the corners live on the axes.

Key pointsL1 = diamond with corners on axes; L2 = sphere, no corners; sparsity is a geometric consequence.

Question 4 · explain

You have 50 correlated predictors all plausibly relevant. Lasso picks one and zeros the rest. What's a better approach?

Model answer

Elastic net. It's an L1 + L2 penalty: the L1 part encourages sparsity, the L2 part has a "grouping effect" that keeps correlated predictors in together rather than arbitrarily killing all but one. Tune the mixing parameter $\alpha$ (1 = lasso, 0 = ridge) by CV. Alternatively, for pure "group" structure (e.g., dummies for one factor), use group lasso to select or zero out whole groups together.

Key pointselastic net: L1 + L2; grouping effect; group lasso when groups are known a priori.
