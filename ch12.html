Chapter 12

Unsupervised Learning

No labels. No "right answer." You're trying to find structure — clusters, directions, associations — without being told what the structure should look like.

Big idea

Supervised learning has a ground truth to score against. Unsupervised learning doesn't — so evaluation becomes an argument, not a number.

PCA compresses many correlated features into a few uncorrelated ones. Clustering groups similar observations together. Both are exploratory by nature — you use them to see, not to predict.

1. Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

You have $p$ correlated features. PCA finds a new set of $p$ axes — principal components — that are uncorrelated with each other, ordered so the first captures the most variance, the second the most remaining variance, and so on.

Imagine a cloud of 3D points that's actually squished into a near-2D pancake. PCA rotates your coordinate system so the first axis lies along the pancake's longest direction, the second along its second-longest, and the third points through the thin part (which you can mostly ignore). You haven't lost information — you've just found the natural axes.

The steps

  1. Standardize each feature (mean 0, variance 1). Required unless all features are on the same scale.
  2. Compute the covariance matrix of the standardized features.
  3. Find its eigenvectors (the new axes) and eigenvalues (how much variance each axis captures).
  4. Order by eigenvalue. The first k eigenvectors are your principal components.

How many components to keep?

Plot the scree plot: eigenvalue vs component number. Look for the "elbow." Or pick enough to explain, say, 90% of variance. Or use cross-validation if you have a downstream supervised task (Ch 6's PCR).

Drag the angle to rotate. PC1 is chosen to maximize the variance of the projection (orange tick marks). The optimal PC1 points along the data's longest axis.

What PCA is not

  • Not variable selection — every component uses all original variables.
  • Not always interpretable. PC1 might be "a little of everything" with no obvious meaning.
  • Not guaranteed to help supervised tasks. High-variance directions ≠ predictive directions (the PCR warning from Ch 6).

2. Missing values via matrix completion

A creative use of PCA. If your data matrix has missing entries (a classic example: user-movie ratings, mostly blank), you can assume the true matrix has low rank (i.e., describable by a few principal components). Fill in the missing values consistent with that low-rank structure.

Iterative algorithm: guess missing values → run PCA → reconstruct matrix from top components → update missing values → repeat. Converges to an imputation. Netflix-style recommender systems are variations on this.

3. K-means clustering

Pre-commit to $K$ clusters. Find $K$ cluster centers and assign each point to the nearest one, so that within-cluster distances are minimized.

The algorithm (Lloyd's)

  1. Randomly initialize $K$ centers.
  2. Assign each point to the nearest center.
  3. Recompute each center as the mean of its assigned points.
  4. Repeat 2–3 until assignments stop changing.

Converges to a local minimum of within-cluster variance. Different starts can give different answers — run it many times and keep the best.

Picking K

K-means will find K clusters whether or not K clusters actually exist. Use:

  • Elbow method: plot within-cluster SS vs K; look for the bend.
  • Silhouette score: measures how similar points are to their own cluster vs other clusters. Higher is better.
  • Gap statistic: compare your WCSS to what you'd get from uniform random data.
K-means assumes spherical, similarly-sized clusters. It fails on elongated clusters, nested clusters, or clusters of very different sizes. Run it and look. Don't trust the label output blindly.

4. Hierarchical clustering

Instead of pre-committing to $K$, build a tree (dendrogram) where the leaves are observations and each internal node merges the most similar pair. You can cut the tree at any level to get any number of clusters.

Linkage methods

To merge clusters, you need a definition of "distance between clusters":

  • Complete linkage: max distance between any pair of points in the two clusters. Compact clusters.
  • Single linkage: min distance. Prone to "chaining" — long stringy clusters.
  • Average linkage: mean pairwise distance. Often a reasonable default.
  • Ward linkage: merges minimize within-cluster variance increase. Spherical, balanced clusters.
Different linkage methods can give completely different dendrograms from the same data. There's no "true" answer — you're picking an assumption. Ward and average are solid defaults.

6. Practical cautions

  • Scale your features. Clustering is distance-based. A variable in thousands will dominate a variable in fractions.
  • Try multiple random starts for K-means.
  • Don't over-interpret cluster labels. The algorithm doesn't know what the clusters mean. You have to interpret them.
  • Clusters can be artifacts. Clustering will produce clusters regardless of whether they exist in a meaningful sense. Always validate on held-out data or downstream use.
  • High dimensions hurt. Distance loses meaning in high dimensions — consider PCA or feature selection first.

Flashcards

1
What does PCA do in one sentence?
Rotates the coordinate system so new axes (principal components) are uncorrelated and ordered by how much variance they capture.
2
Why standardize before PCA?
Variance is scale-dependent. A variable in thousands dominates one in fractions. Standardizing to mean 0, SD 1 makes the components reflect structure, not units.
3
How do you pick the number of components?
Scree plot (look for elbow), cumulative variance threshold (e.g., 90%), or cross-validation if there's a downstream task.
4
Describe K-means in four steps.
1) Pick K random centers. 2) Assign each point to the nearest center. 3) Recompute each center as the mean of its points. 4) Repeat 2-3 until stable.
5
What shapes does K-means assume?
Roughly spherical, similarly-sized clusters. It fails on elongated, nested, or very unbalanced clusters.
6
What is a dendrogram?
A tree showing the hierarchical merging of observations into clusters. Cut it horizontally at any level to choose a number of clusters.
7
Name two linkage methods and one of each's quirks.
Single linkage (min distance) — chains long stringy clusters. Complete (max distance) — compact clusters. Average — reasonable default. Ward — balanced, spherical.
8
Can clustering find clusters that aren't there?
Yes. Every clustering algorithm returns clusters regardless of whether they meaningfully exist. Validate against held-out data, domain knowledge, or downstream use.
9
K-means vs hierarchical — when pick which?
K-means: large n, you've decided on K, spherical clusters expected. Hierarchical: smaller n, you want to explore different cluster counts, non-spherical shapes.
10
Why is high variance not the same as high predictive value?
PCA finds directions of greatest X-variance. Nothing guarantees those directions relate to Y. PCR can miss the predictive signal if it lives in a low-variance direction of X.
