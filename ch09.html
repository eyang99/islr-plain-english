Chapter 9

Support Vector Machines

Draw the widest possible empty street between two classes. The street's middle line is your classifier. Then, with a clever trick, extend the same idea to curved boundaries.

Big idea

Don't just separate the classes. Separate them with the biggest possible margin.

The margin is the distance from the decision boundary to the nearest training points. Maximizing the margin gives a stable classifier that relies only on the "support vectors" — the points sitting on or near the edge of the street.

1. The maximal margin classifier

Suppose two classes are perfectly separable by a straight line (or hyperplane in more dimensions). Many lines work. Which is best?

Answer: the one whose margin — the perpendicular distance from the line to the nearest training point — is as large as possible. Intuitively, a wider buffer zone makes the classifier less sensitive to which exact points it saw.

Imagine you're laying down a road between two neighborhoods that hate each other. You want the road as wide as possible so nobody feels crowded. The houses pressed right against the road edges are the "support vectors" — they define the road width. The rest don't matter.

Limitation

Only works when classes are perfectly separable. In real data they almost never are. So we relax.

2. The soft-margin classifier (Support Vector Classifier)

Allow some points to be on the wrong side of the margin — even on the wrong side of the boundary — and pay a penalty for each violation.

The tradeoff is controlled by a cost parameter $C$ (or equivalently $\lambda$ in some formulations):

  • Large C (small budget for violations): few points allowed across. Narrow margin, highly sensitive to outliers. Low bias, high variance.
  • Small C (generous budget): wider margin, more misclassifications allowed. More bias, less variance.

C is tuned by cross-validation, like every other bias-variance knob.

3. Non-linear boundaries and the kernel trick

What if a straight line can't separate the classes, even with slack? You could manually add polynomial or interaction features, but that explodes quickly in high dimensions.

The kernel trick solves this: it turns out that the SVM optimization only depends on dot products between pairs of training points. If you replace those dot products with a kernel function $K(x_i, x_j)$, you're secretly computing a dot product in a much higher-dimensional space — without ever constructing that space.

The kernels you'll see

  • Linear kernel: $K(x_i, x_j) = x_i^T x_j$. Recovers the standard SVC.
  • Polynomial kernel: $K(x_i, x_j) = (1 + x_i^T x_j)^d$. Captures polynomial decision boundaries of degree d.
  • Radial (RBF) kernel: $K(x_i, x_j) = \exp(-\gamma \|x_i - x_j\|^2)$. The local, flexible default. Infinite-dimensional implicit space.
Without the kernel trick: "lift my 2D data into 100D, fit a line there." With the kernel trick: "never actually construct the 100D space — just compute pairwise similarities using a clever function, and the math works out the same." A rare genuinely slick trick in stats.

4. The RBF kernel, and what γ does

$\gamma$ controls how fast similarity between points drops with distance.

  • Small γ: far-away points still influence each other. Smoother, more linear-looking boundary. Higher bias, lower variance.
  • Large γ: only very close points matter. Boundary wraps tightly around individual points. Lower bias, higher variance, overfitting risk.

RBF-SVM has two hyperparameters (C and γ); both tune by cross-validation, usually on a grid.

5. SVM vs logistic regression

They're closer than they look. Both can be written as "minimize a loss + regularization." The difference is the loss function:

  • Logistic regression: log loss. Every point contributes, always.
  • SVM: hinge loss. Points safely on the correct side of the margin contribute zero. Only boundary points (support vectors) shape the model.

Consequences:

  • SVMs are often more stable when classes are well-separated (only boundary points matter, outliers don't pull the fit).
  • Logistic regression outputs well-calibrated probabilities out of the box. SVMs don't — you have to add Platt scaling or isotonic regression for probabilities.
  • For almost-linearly-separable data, SVM often wins a hair. For most applied problems, logistic regression with regularization is competitive and simpler.
In the deep learning era, SVMs aren't the workhorse they were in 2005. They're still great when n is modest, you have structured features, and you care about stability. For big messy data, trees or neural nets usually win.

6. Multi-class SVM

SVMs are born two-class. To do K classes you fit many binary SVMs:

  • One-vs-one: $\binom{K}{2}$ pairwise classifiers, then vote.
  • One-vs-all: K classifiers, each "class k vs everything else," then take the most confident.

Most software does one of these for you. Performance is usually similar.

Flashcards

1
What is the margin in an SVM?
The perpendicular distance from the decision boundary to the nearest training point. SVMs pick the boundary that maximizes it.
2
What are "support vectors"?
The training points on the edge of the margin (or inside it, for soft-margin). They're the only points that determine the decision boundary. Moving other points doesn't change the fit.
3
What does the cost parameter C control?
How much margin violation is tolerated. Large C = strict, narrow margin, low bias / high variance. Small C = lenient, wide margin, more bias / less variance.
4
Explain the kernel trick in one sentence.
Replace dot products with a kernel function, which implicitly computes a dot product in a much higher-dimensional feature space — without ever building that space.
5
What does γ do in an RBF kernel?
Controls how quickly similarity decays with distance. Large γ = only very close points matter (wiggly boundary, overfit risk). Small γ = far points still interact (smoother).
6
SVM vs logistic regression — core loss difference?
Logistic uses log loss (every point contributes). SVM uses hinge loss (only boundary points contribute; safe points cost zero).
7
Why don't SVMs give probabilities natively?
They output a signed distance to the boundary, not a probability. You need Platt scaling or isotonic regression to turn those into probabilities.
8
Two strategies for multi-class SVM?
One-vs-one: fit K(K−1)/2 pairwise classifiers, vote. One-vs-all: K classifiers (each class vs rest), most confident wins.

Quiz

The widest street between two classes, and the trick for curving it.

Question 1 · compute (kernel)

For the polynomial kernel $K(x, y) = (x \cdot y + 1)^2$ with $x = (1, 2)$ and $y = (3, 4)$, compute $K(x, y)$.

Solution

$x \cdot y = 1 \cdot 3 + 2 \cdot 4 = 11$. Then $K = (11 + 1)^2 = 12^2 = \mathbf{144}$. The kernel trick's power is that you never form the 5-dimensional feature map (all degree-≤2 monomials); you only need this inner product.

Question 2 · explain

Two training points sit in the middle of your class blob, well inside the margin. You delete them. Does your SVM change? Now delete one of the support vectors. What happens?

Model answer

Deleting points well inside the margin changes nothing — they don't enter the dual solution and have zero Lagrange multipliers. The decision boundary depends only on support vectors (points on the margin or misclassified). Delete a support vector, and the boundary typically shifts, sometimes meaningfully, because you've removed a constraint that was actively shaping the hyperplane. This is the SVM's signature property: the model is fully determined by a sparse set of "boundary cases," not by the bulk of the training data — which is both a strength (elegant sparsity) and a weakness (sensitive to the few points that happen to sit on the boundary).

Key pointsinterior points don't matter; support vectors are the whole model; that sparsity is both feature and liability.

Question 3 · explain

You grid-search $C$ and $\gamma$ on an RBF SVM. $C = 100$, $\gamma = 10$ gives zero training error but terrible CV error. What's going on, and which knob do you adjust first?

Model answer

Classic overfitting from a too-flexible kernel. Large $\gamma$ makes each RBF bump narrow, so the model can carve a tiny Gaussian around every training point. Large $C$ reinforces this by penalizing any margin violation heavily. Together: zero training error, no generalization. The first knob to turn down is $\gamma$ — shrinking it widens the bumps and smooths the boundary. Then reconsider $C$. Rule of thumb: a sensible $\gamma$ is near $1 / (p \cdot \text{Var}(X))$ (scikit-learn's default scale). Grid-search on a log scale.

Key pointslarge $\gamma$ = narrow bumps = overfit; drop $\gamma$ first; grid on log scale.
