Chapter 3

Linear Regression

The oldest, simplest, most over-deployed model in the world. Master this and you understand a surprising amount of ML.

Big idea

Draw the best-fitting straight line (or hyperplane) through your points.

"Best" = "minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances between the line and the points." That's it. Every insight in this chapter is a consequence of that one choice.

1. Simple linear regression (one predictor)

Assume $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \epsilon$. Your job: estimate $\beta_0$ (intercept) and $\beta_1$ (slope) from data.

The method is least squares: pick $\hat{\beta}_0, \hat{\beta}_1$ to minimize

$$ \mathrm{RSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i)^2 $$

RSS = residual sum of squares. A residual is a vertical distance from a point to the line. We square them (so negatives don't cancel and big misses are penalized more) and sum them up.

Drag the sliders. Watch RSS change. The "snap" button solves for the minimum in closed form.

Closed-form coefficients

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \qquad \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

Slope = covariance divided by variance of X. Intercept makes the line pass through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

2. Reading the coefficients

$\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.8$ means: "for every 1-unit increase in $X$, $Y$ is predicted to increase by 0.8 units, holding everything else constant." The word "predicted" is doing work — this is a fitted relationship, not a proven cause.

Are the coefficients real?

Each coefficient has a standard error (how much the estimate would bounce around under different samples). From SE we get:

  • Confidence interval: $\hat{\beta} \pm 2 \cdot SE(\hat{\beta})$ is an ~95% CI.
  • t-statistic: $t = \hat{\beta} / SE(\hat{\beta})$. How many SEs is the estimate from zero?
  • p-value: P(seeing a t this extreme | true $\beta = 0$). Small p = the slope is probably not zero.
p < 0.05 on a coefficient means "probably not zero," not "important" or "causal." Large datasets produce tiny p-values on economically meaningless effects. Always look at effect size too.

3. Multiple linear regression

$Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \dots + \beta_p X_p + \epsilon$. Same idea, more predictors. Each $\hat{\beta}_j$ is the effect of $X_j$ after accounting for all other predictors in the model.

In simple regression, you're checking if ice cream sales predict drownings. Strong relationship! In multiple regression, you add "temperature" as another predictor. Now ice cream's coefficient collapses. The effect you saw was really temperature, showing up through ice cream.

The coefficient changes meaning

A predictor's coefficient in simple regression is rarely its coefficient in multiple regression. Adding predictors can flip signs, kill significance, or reveal something new. This is why "correlation ≠ causation" becomes "conditional correlation ≠ causation" in ML.

Testing whether ANY predictor matters: the F-test

The F-statistic tests the joint hypothesis that all coefficients (other than intercept) are zero. If F is large (and p small), at least one predictor matters. It's a gatekeeper — you run it first before trusting individual t-tests.

4. Qualitative predictors (dummy variables)

Linear regression only speaks numbers. For a category like "region = {North, South, East, West}," you create dummy variables: 0/1 columns for each category (minus one, to avoid redundancy). The coefficients become "how much does South differ from the reference category, North?"

The dropped category is the reference — all other coefficients are relative to it. If you drop "North," the intercept is the North average, and "South coefficient" = South - North.

5. Interactions & non-linearity, lightly

Standard linear regression assumes effects are additive (each predictor's contribution is independent of the others) and linear (a straight-line relationship).

  • Interaction terms (X1 * X2): let the effect of X1 depend on the value of X2. "TV ads help, but only if you also advertise on radio."
  • Polynomial terms (X², X³): let the relationship curve. Still "linear" in the model's eyes because the coefficients multiply fixed transformations of X.

These give you a lot of mileage before you need fancier methods (Ch 7).

6. Diagnostics: when linear regression lies

Linear regression makes assumptions. Violating them doesn't crash the model — it just makes the output misleading. Always check:

If your variables have huge VIFs (>10), your individual coefficient estimates are unstable even if the overall model predicts fine. You can still predict, but don't trust the "which predictor matters" story.

7. R²: the model's self-graded report card

$R^2 = 1 - \frac{RSS}{TSS}$ where TSS is total sum of squares (how much Y varies overall). $R^2 = 0.7$ means "my model explains 70% of the variation in Y."

$R^2$ always goes up when you add predictors, even garbage ones. Use adjusted R² for fair comparison across models, or better, use cross-validation (Ch 5).

8. Linear regression vs KNN

KNN (Ch 2) is flexible and makes no form assumption. Linear regression is rigid and assumes a straight line. When is each better?

  • True relationship is roughly linear → linear regression wins. Fewer parameters, lower variance.
  • Strongly non-linear truth + lots of data + low dimension → KNN wins.
  • High dimensions (p large) → linear regression wins, because KNN's curse-of-dimensionality is brutal.

Takeaway: "simpler" wins more often than you'd expect, especially in high dimensions.

9. The mandatory warning label

Charles Wheelan (Naked Statistics) calls regression "the hydrogen bomb of the statistics arsenal" — enormously powerful, easy to misuse, and capable of doing real damage in the wrong hands.

The estrogen story. Through the 1990s, a 122,000-person observational study (the Nurses' Health Study) showed women on estrogen supplements had one-third the heart attacks. By 2001, ~15 million women were on hormone replacement therapy. Then randomized clinical trials ran and showed the opposite — estrogen raised risk of heart disease, stroke, blood clots, and breast cancer. New York Times Magazine's estimate of premature deaths and serious adverse events from a regression-driven recommendation: "tens of thousands." The rule isn't "regression is bad." It's: a regression coefficient is a correlation, not a cause. Clinical trials are the correction.

Here are six traps that do most of the damage:

i. Fitting a line to something that isn't a line

Regression returns a single slope even when the true relationship bends. The computer never complains. You get a confident answer for a question the data can't answer.

A warning label for regression would read: Do not use when the underlying relationship isn't linear. It's the hair dryer in the bathtub — the appliance works, you just shouldn't use it here.

ii. Correlation vs causation (spurious edition)

Include Chinese per-capita income as a predictor of US autism rates and you'll get a strong, "statistically significant" positive coefficient. Both have been rising for twenty years. Neither causes the other. Any two trending series will produce a fake relationship.

iii. Reverse causality

A regression finds that more golf lessons predict worse scores. Does taking lessons ruin your swing? More likely: you sign up for lessons because you're playing poorly. The arrow points the other way. When you can't justify the direction from outside the data, the coefficient is a ouija board.

Don't use as an explanatory variable anything that might be caused by the thing you're trying to explain. "Does reducing unemployment boost GDP?" is a trap — GDP growth is what reduces unemployment.

iv. Omitted variable bias

Run a regression of test scores on school spending. You'll find a big, significant positive coefficient. Policy implication: spend more, scores go up. But wealthy districts spend more and send kids with educated parents to school. Leave parental education out of the equation and "school spending" picks up its effect — you're measuring two things, reporting one.

The same trap explains "golfers get more heart disease" headlines: old people play more golf; old people get more heart disease. Control for age and the effect reverses.

An omitted variable inside the coefficient is the single most common way a regression lies. The fix is domain knowledge, not bigger data. Bigger data just gives you a more confidently wrong coefficient.

v. Extrapolating beyond the data

A regression fit on adults is only valid for adults. Wheelan's model, trained on the Changing Lives dataset of grown-ups, predicts that his newborn daughter should have weighed −19.6 pounds. (She weighed 8½.) The model wasn't broken; it was being asked a question nothing in its training set could answer.

In ML language: your model is only valid over the support of its training distribution. Outside that support, predictions aren't "uncertain" — they're unjustified.

vi. Data mining (too many knobs)

Shove 50 predictors into a regression with no theory and one or two will cross p < 0.05 by pure chance. Flip enough coins and someone in the room will flip six tails in a row — not because they're talented, but because you ran the test a lot.

John Ioannidis, reviewing 49 of the most-cited medical studies in top journals, found roughly one-third were later refuted. His estimate for biomedical research overall: about half of published findings are wrong. Chapter 13 is the honest answer to this problem.

Designing the regression matters more than the math. Think hard about what variables belong, what's missing, which direction the arrow runs. The arithmetic is the easy part.

Flashcards

1
Define least squares.
The method of estimating coefficients by minimizing the sum of squared residuals (vertical distances from points to the line).
2
What does β₁ = 0.8 mean in multiple regression?
A 1-unit increase in X₁ is associated with a 0.8-unit predicted increase in Y, holding all other predictors constant. The "holding constant" part is crucial.
3
What does the F-test check?
The joint null that ALL coefficients (except intercept) are zero. Small p = at least one predictor is useful. Run before trusting individual t-tests.
4
What does the standard error of a coefficient measure?
How much the estimated coefficient would bounce around across different samples. Small SE = stable estimate. CI ≈ β̂ ± 2·SE.
5
Why does adding a predictor sometimes flip another's sign?
Because a coefficient means "effect AFTER accounting for other predictors." A new variable can absorb the effect that was previously being attributed to the old one (confounding).
6
What is a dummy variable?
A 0/1 column representing a category. For k categories, you create k−1 dummies (the dropped one is the reference). Coefficients are "difference from the reference."
7
What is an interaction term?
A term like X₁·X₂ that lets the effect of X₁ depend on X₂. "The slope changes depending on the other variable."
8
Name three linear regression assumption violations.
Non-linearity, heteroscedasticity (non-constant error variance), correlated errors, outliers, high leverage, collinearity.
9
Why is R² misleading for model comparison?
It only goes up (or stays flat) when you add predictors — even useless ones. Use adjusted R² or cross-validated test error.
10
Linear regression vs KNN — when does each win?
Linear wins when truth is ~linear, data is limited, or p is large. KNN wins when truth is nonlinear and you have lots of data in low dimensions.
11
What does collinearity break?
Coefficient estimates — they become unstable (huge SEs) because the model can't separate which correlated predictor is doing the work. Predictions still OK.

Quiz

Regression looks simple. The traps are everywhere.

Question 1 · compute (β̂₁)

Given $\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) = 48$ and $\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 = 12$, find the least-squares slope $\hat{\beta}_1$.

Solution

$\hat{\beta}_1 = \dfrac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \dfrac{48}{12} = \mathbf{4}$.

Question 2 · compute (β̂₀)

Using $\hat{\beta}_1 = 4$ from above, and $\bar{x} = 5$, $\bar{y} = 10$: find the intercept $\hat{\beta}_0$.

Solution

$\hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} = 10 - 4 \cdot 5 = \mathbf{-10}$. The line is $\hat{y} = -10 + 4x$. It passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y}) = (5, 10)$, as the closed-form always does.

Question 3 · explain

Your regression output says ad spend has $\hat{\beta} = 2.3$, $SE = 0.1$, $p < 0.001$, and $R^2 = 0.02$. Is ad spend "important"?

Model answer

Two different questions. Statistically: yes — the t-statistic is 23 and p < 0.001, so the coefficient is very precisely estimated and clearly nonzero. Practically: no — R² = 0.02 means ad spend explains 2% of the variation in the outcome. The effect is real but tiny. Large-sample datasets routinely produce microscopic p-values on economically meaningless effects. Always quote the effect size alongside significance, and use $R^2$ or an equivalent measure of practical magnitude.

Key pointssignificance ≠ importance; huge $n$ makes tiny effects look significant; always report effect size.

Question 4 · explain

You add "is_weekend" to a model and the coefficient on "day_of_month" reverses. What's going on?

Model answer

Confounding / omitted variable bias. The original "day_of_month" coefficient was picking up part of the weekend effect (since weekends cluster on certain days of the month). Once is_weekend is in the model, "day_of_month" now means "effect of day-of-month holding weekend status constant" — a different estimand from before, and apparently one with the opposite sign. This is a clean demonstration that coefficients aren't stable properties of the predictor; they're properties of the model specification.

Key pointsomitted variable bias; coefficients depend on what else is in the model; "holding other things constant" is literal.

Question 5 · explain

Your residuals vs fitted plot shows a smile (residuals curve upward at both ends). Which assumption is broken and what do you try?

Model answer

Linearity is broken. A smile in the residuals means the true relationship bends upward at the extremes; your straight line systematically under-predicts there and over-predicts in the middle. Fixes, in order: add a quadratic term ($X^2$) or a more flexible transform ($\log$, $\sqrt{\cdot}$), fit a spline (Ch 7), or step up to a non-linear method. A single-slope coefficient cannot summarize a bending relationship — see the "mandatory warning label" above.

Key pointsnon-linear residual pattern = non-linearity; quadratic/spline fixes; hair-dryer-in-bathtub rule.
